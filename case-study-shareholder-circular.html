Case Study

Consolidating Shareholder Data for a TSX-Filed M&A Circular

The Results

Metric Result
Holders processed ~1,400 across shareholders, warrants, options, COC, and LOAN records
Time saved Weeks of manual reconciliation compressed into hours per rebuild cycle
Deadline TSX-filed circular delivered on time
Data reach Addresses reconciled across 21 international jurisdictions against an external geocoder
Deliverables Consolidated mailing workbook, TSX-formatted multi-tab workbook, shareholder database

The Challenge

A TSX-listed Canadian issuer was preparing a shareholder circular for an M&A transaction. The filing — and the mailing behind it — had to be right, and it had to be on time. Missed ballots on a TSX circular are not a fix-it-tomorrow problem.

The underlying data was the problem. Shareholder information lived across three unrelated sources: a registry export from the transfer agent, an internal cap-table workbook maintained by the company's advisors, and a scattered email-address spreadsheet. Addresses were inconsistent across twenty-one jurisdictions — fuzzy city names, missing postal codes, UK and Irish cities that external geocoders had silently mapped to the wrong country, and edge territories like the Falkland Islands, Gibraltar, and Guernsey that off-the-shelf tools don't handle well. Some holders appeared in multiple forms, some held different instruments (shares, warrants, options) that needed to be combined onto a single envelope, and a handful required two envelopes by explicit client direction.

None of it could be trusted for a regulated mailing without a full reconciliation — and there wasn't time to do that reconciliation by hand.

The Solution

I was brought in to consolidate the data and deliver a circular the company could file with confidence. The approach was full-stack: clean the inputs, build the system, ship the artifacts.

Address cleanup pipeline. Built a fuzzy city matcher and a jurisdiction-aware fix layer (particularly for UK and Irish cities that off-the-shelf geocoders mishandle), then audited every holder's address against the corrected geocoder output. The addresses that went on envelopes were the addresses that would actually deliver.

Consolidated mailing list. Reduced the raw data to one row per holder by default, with explicit multi-envelope exceptions where the client required them — shares split proportionally and the exception flagged for review. No silent duplicates, no guessed merges.

TSX-formatted multi-tab deliverable. A single workbook with five tabs — Shareholders, Warrants, Options, COC, LOAN — structured to the filing's requirements, with warrants and options split per TSX Trust's handling rules. Versioned through two dozen iterations as the transaction terms evolved.

Shareholder database. A working register of the full shareholder base delivered as a standalone artifact alongside the circular deliverables.

All driven by Python and Excel automation that preserved the quirks of the real workbooks (hidden sheets, formula-bearing cells, formatting the client relied on) rather than flattening them into something cleaner but wrong.

Key Takeaway

This is the shape of a lot of business operations: the data exists, but it lives across three systems that were never designed to talk to each other, and when a deadline hits, someone has to reconcile it by hand. That works until it doesn't — until the stakes are a regulated filing, the volume is 1,400 holders across 21 jurisdictions, and there's no time left. Clarys's job in a project like this is to understand the operation end-to-end, build the pipeline that does the reconciliation reliably, and hand the client artifacts they can sign their name to. The circular got filed on time, the workbooks match, the mailing delivered.
